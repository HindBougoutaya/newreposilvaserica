Complete Mulberry Cultivation Guide

Growing healthy mulberry trees for sustainable silkworm farming

Introduction to Mulberry Trees

Mulberry trees (Morus spp.) are fast-growing, deciduous trees that produce the essential food for silkworms. They're hardy, drought-resistant once established, and can thrive in various soil conditions. For silkworm cultivation, the white mulberry (Morus alba) is preferred because its leaves are most nutritious for Bombyx mori silkworms.

In our Silva Serica project, each family plants 5-10 mulberry trees, creating both a food source for silkworms and a greener environment for the community. The trees also improve air quality and provide shade.

Planting Guidelines

1. Climate Requirements

  • Temperature: Optimal growth between 24-30¬∞C (75-86¬∞F)
  • Rainfall: 600-2500 mm annually, but can tolerate drought
  • Altitude: Grows well from sea level to 4000 feet
  • Sunlight: Full sun (6-8 hours daily) for best leaf production

2. Soil Preparation

  1. Choose well-drained soil with pH 6.0-7.0
  2. Dig planting holes 60x60x60 cm (2x2x2 feet)
  3. Mix soil with 5-10 kg of well-rotted manure or compost
  4. Space trees 3-4 meters apart for optimal growth
  5. For hedge planting, space 1-2 meters apart

3. Planting Process

  • Best planting time: Early spring or start of rainy season
  • Water thoroughly after planting
  • Apply mulch around base to retain moisture
  • Protect young trees from strong winds

Care and Maintenance

Watering Schedule

First year: Water every 3-4 days during dry periods
Established trees: Deep watering every 10-14 days in dry season
Important: Reduce watering before leaf harvest to increase leaf quality

Fertilization

  • Spring: Apply balanced fertilizer (NPK 10:10:10)
  • After each harvest: Apply nitrogen-rich fertilizer
  • Annually: Add compost or manure in early spring
  • For poor soils: Add micronutrients (zinc, iron) as needed

Pruning Techniques

  1. Formative pruning (Year 1-2): Shape tree for easy harvesting
  2. Maintenance pruning: Remove dead/diseased branches annually
  3. Harvest pruning: Cut branches to encourage new growth
  4. Height control: Keep trees at 2-3 meters for easy leaf collection

Harvesting Mulberry Leaves

When to Harvest

  • First harvest: 6-8 months after planting
  • Peak season: Spring and summer months
  • Best time of day: Early morning when leaves are crisp
  • Frequency: Every 45-60 days during growing season

Harvesting Methods

  1. Leaf picking: Hand-pick mature leaves (for small scale)
  2. Branch cutting: Cut entire branches (most common method)
  3. Whole tree harvest: Cut back entire tree (for intensive farming)

Leaf Storage

  • Store in cool, shaded area with good air circulation
  • Use within 24 hours for best nutritional value
  • For longer storage, refrigerate at 4¬∞C (39¬∞F) for up to 3 days
  • Never store leaves in plastic bags (causes condensation)

Pest and Disease Management

Common Pests

  • Mulberry scale: Use neem oil spray
  • Leaf rollers: Hand-pick or use biological controls
  • Aphids: Spray with soapy water solution
  • Whiteflies: Yellow sticky traps

Disease Prevention

  1. Powdery mildew: Ensure good air circulation
  2. Leaf spot: Remove affected leaves promptly
  3. Root rot: Avoid waterlogged soil
  4. Bacterial blight: Use disease-resistant varieties

Organic Control Methods

We recommend organic methods to ensure leaves are safe for silkworms:

  • Companion planting with marigolds or garlic
  • Regular inspection and manual removal of pests
  • Maintaining tree health through proper nutrition
  • Using botanical extracts (neem, garlic, chili)

Yield Expectations

Tree Age Annual Leaf Yield Silkworms Supported
Year 1 2-3 kg 500-750 worms
Year 2 8-12 kg 2,000-3,000 worms
Year 3+ 15-25 kg 4,000-6,000 worms

Community Planting Tips

  • Organize group planting days to build community spirit
  • Create a shared nursery for saplings
  • Establish a rotation system for leaf harvesting
  • Share knowledge about pest management
  • Document growth progress in community meetings

Silkworm Care & Adoption Guide

Raising healthy Bombyx mori silkworms from eggs to cocoons

Introduction to Silkworm Farming

The silkworm (Bombyx mori) has been domesticated for over 5,000 years. These remarkable creatures transform mulberry leaves into luxurious silk. Raising silkworms teaches patience, responsibility, and respect for nature while providing economic opportunities. This guide covers everything from obtaining eggs to harvesting cocoons.

In the Silva Serica project, each family starts with 100-200 silkworms, learning the basics before potentially scaling up. The process takes about 45 days from egg to cocoon.

Getting Started

Essential Equipment

  • Rearing trays: Shallow boxes with ventilation (wood or plastic)
  • Cleaning tools: Soft brushes, tweezers, and fine mesh
  • Thermometer and hygrometer: For monitoring conditions
  • Feeding racks: For organizing mulberry leaves
  • Mounting structures: For cocoon spinning (paper cones, twigs)

Environment Setup

  1. Choose a clean, dry room away from pesticides
  2. Maintain temperature: 24-28¬∞C (75-82¬∞F)
  3. Keep humidity: 70-80% for young worms, 60-70% for mature
  4. Ensure good ventilation without direct drafts
  5. Provide indirect natural light (avoid direct sunlight)

Lifecycle Management

Stage 1: Eggs (10-14 days)

  • Source disease-free eggs from reputable suppliers
  • Store at 5¬∞C (41¬∞F) until ready to hatch
  • Incubate at 25¬∞C (77¬∞F) with 80-85% humidity
  • Eggs will darken to blue-gray before hatching
  • Disinfect with 2% formaldehyde solution if needed

Stage 2: Larvae - 5 Instars (25-28 days)

First Instar (1-3 days):

  • Feed tender young leaves chopped finely
  • Maintain 28¬∞C (82¬∞F) and 85% humidity
  • Clean trays daily after worms move to fresh leaves

Second & Third Instar (6-9 days):

  • Feed whole young leaves
  • Increase space as worms grow
  • Temperature: 26-27¬∞C (79-81¬∞F)

Fourth & Fifth Instar (15-18 days):

  • Feed mature leaves 3-4 times daily
  • Worms eat voraciously - ensure constant supply
  • Clean trays twice daily due to high waste
  • Temperature: 24-25¬∞C (75-77¬∞F)

Stage 3: Spinning & Cocoon Formation (3-4 days)

  1. Worms stop eating and seek mounting place
  2. Provide spinning structures (paper cones, twigs)
  3. Maintain 23-24¬∞C (73-75¬∞F) and 65% humidity
  4. Do not disturb during spinning process

Stage 4: Pupation & Moth Emergence (14-18 days)

  • Cocoons complete after 3 days of spinning
  • Harvest cocoons 7-8 days after completion
  • For breeding: Allow moths to emerge naturally
  • For silk: Process cocoons before moths emerge

Feeding Guidelines

Leaf Selection

  • Use fresh, pesticide-free mulberry leaves
  • Young worms: Tender leaves from tree top
  • Mature worms: Mature leaves from middle branches
  • Avoid wet, dusty, or contaminated leaves
  • Never use leaves from roadsides (pollution risk)

Feeding Schedule

Instar Feeds/Day Leaf Amount Timing
1st 4-5 1-2 leaves/100 worms Every 4-5 hours
2nd-3rd 3-4 Increasing amounts Every 6 hours
4th-5th 3-4 Ad libitum (free feeding) Morning, noon, evening

Health Management

Common Health Issues

  • Grasserie (Viral): Worms become sluggish, turn yellowish. Isolate immediately.
  • Flacherie (Bacterial): Diarrhea, slow growth. Improve hygiene.
  • Muscardine (Fungal): White growth on dead worms. Control humidity.
  • Pebrine (Protozoan): Irregular growth, black spots. Destroy infected stock.

Prevention Strategies

  1. Maintain strict hygiene - wash hands, disinfect tools
  2. Provide fresh, clean leaves only
  3. Control temperature and humidity precisely
  4. Remove sick/dead worms immediately
  5. Clean trays thoroughly between batches

Natural Remedies

  • Add neem leaves to feed for antibacterial properties
  • Use garlic extract spray for fungal prevention
  • Place lime powder around rearing area to deter pests
  • Ensure good air circulation to prevent disease

Community Adoption Program

Adoption Process

  1. Orientation: Attend Silva Serica workshop
  2. Starter Kit: Receive 100 eggs, rearing tray, guide
  3. Training: Learn basic care in hands-on session
  4. Support: Weekly check-ins with community mentor
  5. Harvest: Return cocoons for processing or keep for personal use

Success Tips for New Families

  • Start small (100-200 worms) to gain experience
  • Involve children - teaches responsibility and biology
  • Keep a simple journal to track growth and issues
  • Share experiences at weekly community meetings
  • Celebrate milestones (first cocoon, first harvest)

Expected Outcomes

With proper care, 100 healthy silkworms will produce:

  • Approximately 1 kg of fresh cocoons
  • 150-200 grams of raw silk after processing
  • Enough silk for 1-2 scarves or small garments
  • Potential income: $20-40 depending on quality
  • Valuable experience for scaling up next season

Ethical Considerations

  • Handle worms gently - they are living creatures
  • Provide optimal conditions throughout lifecycle
  • If breeding, ensure moths have space to emerge and mate
  • For silk production, use humane harvesting methods
  • Respect the silkworm's contribution to your livelihood

Silk Processing Techniques Guide

Traditional and modern methods for transforming cocoons into luxurious silk

Introduction to Silk Processing

Silk processing transforms cocoons into usable thread through a series of steps: harvesting, stifling, reeling, twisting, and dyeing. This guide covers both traditional methods suitable for community production and modern techniques for higher quality. With practice, families can produce silk thread ready for weaving or sale.

The Silva Serica project establishes community processing centers where families can bring their cocoons for collective processing, sharing equipment and knowledge.

Cocoon Harvest & Preparation

When to Harvest

  • Harvest 7-8 days after cocoon completion
  • Cocoons should feel firm and dry
  • For silk: Harvest before moth emerges (pierces cocoon)
  • For breeding: Leave until moths emerge naturally
  • Best time: Morning after dew has evaporated

Sorting and Grading

  1. First grade: Perfect shape, firm, uniform color
  2. Second grade: Slightly misshapen but intact
  3. Third grade: Double cocoons, thin areas
  4. Rejects: Pierced, damaged, or stained cocoons

Stifling Methods (Killing the Pupae)

Sun drying (traditional):

  • Spread cocoons in thin layer in direct sunlight
  • Turn regularly for 2-3 days
  • Advantage: No equipment needed
  • Disadvantage: Weather dependent, can discolor silk

Steam stifling (recommended):

  • Use steamer or large pot with rack
  • Steam for 15-20 minutes at 100¬∞C (212¬∞F)
  • Advantage: Preserves silk quality, kills quickly
  • Disadvantage: Requires careful temperature control

Hot air drying (modern):

  • Use oven at 90-100¬∞C (194-212¬∞F) for 1-2 hours
  • Advantage: Consistent results
  • Disadvantage: Equipment cost

Traditional Reeling Methods

Basic Hand Reeling Setup

  • Basin: Large pot or basin for hot water
  • Brush: Soft bristle brush to find filament ends
  • Reel: Simple hand-cranked reel or rotating frame
  • Heat source: Stove or fire to maintain water at 80-85¬∞C (176-185¬∞F)
  • Guidance system: Porcelain eyelets or smooth guides

Step-by-Step Reeling Process

  1. Softening: Soak cocoons in hot water (80¬∞C/176¬∞F) for 10-15 minutes
  2. Brushing: Gently brush cocoon surface to find filament end
  3. Threading: Attach 5-10 filament ends together (for strength)
  4. Reeling: Wind combined filaments onto reel slowly and evenly
  5. Joining: As cocoons finish, add new ones to maintain continuity
  6. Drying: Allow reeled silk to dry on reel before removal

Common Issues & Solutions

Problem Cause Solution
Breakages Water too hot, rough handling Lower temperature, handle gently
Uneven thread Inconsistent reeling speed Maintain steady pace, use guides
Sticky silk Sericin not properly softened Increase soaking time/temperature
Weak thread Too few filaments combined Use 8-10 filaments for strength

Twisting and Plying

Why Twist Silk Thread?

  • Increases strength and durability
  • Creates different textures (crepe, tram, organzine)
  • Prevents unraveling during weaving
  • Allows blending with other fibers

Traditional Twisting Methods

Hand spindle:

  • Simple wooden spindle with weight
  • Spin while feeding thread from reel
  • Creates Z-twist (right-handed) or S-twist (left-handed)
  • Portable - can be done anywhere

Spinning wheel:

  • Foot-powered or hand-cranked wheel
  • More consistent twist than spindle
  • Faster production rate
  • Can ply multiple threads together

Plying Techniques

  1. Singles: Single twisted strand - for weft threads
  2. 2-ply: Two singles twisted together - balanced, strong
  3. 3-ply: Three singles - very strong for warp threads
  4. Cable: Ply already plied threads - for heavy fabrics

Natural Dyeing Techniques

Mordanting (Preparing Silk for Dye)

  • Alum: Most common, produces bright colors
  • Iron: Darkens colors, creates gray/black tones
  • Tin: Brightens colors, especially reds/yellows
  • Copper: Creates greenish tones

Local Natural Dye Sources

Color Plant Source Part Used Mordant
Yellow/Gold Turmeric, onion skins Root, skins Alum
Red/Pink Madder root, pomegranate Root, rind Alum or tin
Blue Indigo, woad Leaves None needed
Brown Walnut hulls, tea Hulls, leaves Iron
Green Nettle, spinach Leaves Copper

Basic Dyeing Process

  1. Prepare dye bath by simmering plant material 1-2 hours
  2. Strain to remove plant material
  3. Add pre-mordanted, wet silk to dye bath
  4. Simmer gently (don't boil) for 30-60 minutes
  5. Remove silk, rinse in cool water until water runs clear
  6. Hang to dry in shade (sunlight fades natural dyes)
